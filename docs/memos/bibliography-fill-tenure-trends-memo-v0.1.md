## 1. Purpose

Scoping v0.2 §12 declared a bibliography gap, flagged by the Architect review: the Mannheim (1928) → present coverage on generational replacement in organisations was thin. Without that fill, claims about cohort-replacement timescales and tenure-trend variance (load-bearing for both H₀-L₅ and H₁) cannot be situated against a contemporary empirical literature.

This memo fills that gap with 15 annotated sources spanning four bands:
- Foundational (Mannheim 1928 plus institutional-timescale anchors).
- Organisational ecology and succession.
- CEO and executive tenure (with one practitioner data series).
- Legislator and political tenure.
- Cohort replacement in scientific fields.

Each entry is annotated with what the source contributes to the programme and which hypothesis or method it bears on. The bibliography is candidate for incorporation into v0.3 §12 after Verifier review.

## 2. Search strategy declaration (TC-11 risk register)

The TC-11 card flags selection bias as the primary risk in literature review. The strategy used for v0.1 of this fill was:

- **Seed set.** Three foundational works named in scoping v0.2 §12 (Mannheim, Olson, North) were retained as anchor points.
- **Domain branches.** Four target domains were identified by the Architect bibliography gap: organisational ecology / succession; CEO and executive tenure; legislator tenure; cohort-replacement timescales in scientific fields.
- **Per-branch criterion.** For each branch, prefer (a) canonical works that established the branch's empirical question, and (b) at least one contemporary empirical work (post-2000) carrying current data. Avoid (c) review-of-reviews where primary work is available; avoid (d) advocacy or branded-prescription literature (already covered as primary sources for T1 in the existing scoping bibliography).
- **Geographic and disciplinary range.** US-centric for tenure data (where the longest series exist), but with a stated awareness that the Catholic-councils empirical pilot (TC-01) is a non-US, non-corporate test site and that comparative breadth will be needed for v0.2 of this fill.
- **Verifier-pass mandate.** The v0.1 list emphasises canonical sources that are widely catalogued and cross-referenced; the Verifier pass should expand the contemporary-empirical end (post-2015 work on tenure variance, cohort effects, and AI-mediated coordination disruption) where the v0.1 coverage is admittedly thin.

Known limits of v0.1: the European institutional-history literature (e.g., on European parliamentary tenure series, on religious-order succession patterns) is under-represented. The Verifier should treat this as a known gap, not a settled coverage.

## 3. Annotated entries

### A. Foundational

**[1] Mannheim, Karl. 1928.** "Das Problem der Generationen." *Kölner Vierteljahreshefte für Soziologie* 7: 157–185, 309–330.

The seed text. Distinguishes biological generation, generational location (a structural-temporal position), and generational unit (the consciously-bound subset that takes its location as a basis for action). Mannheim's central insight — that generational replacement is not biological mortality but the slower production of generational consciousness through historical contact at formative ages — is the conceptual ancestor of the L₅ legitimacy-transfer-generation definition adopted in scoping v0.2 §3. **Bears on:** H₀ definitional grounding.

**[2] Olson, Mancur. 1982.** *The Rise and Decline of Nations: Economic Growth, Stagflation, and Social Rigidities*. New Haven: Yale University Press.

Argues that long-stable polities accumulate distributional coalitions (interest groups capturing rents through institutional sclerosis), and that the timescale of accumulation is roughly generational. The thesis predicts a kind of L₅-bound institutional drag that is *not* technologisable: distributional-coalition accumulation depends on social trust formation at generational scale. **Bears on:** H₀ (sclerosis as a generationally-paced process); H₁ (technological pressure may not relax the floor but may exacerbate the consequences of failing to clear coalitions).

**[3] North, Douglass C. 1990.** *Institutions, Institutional Change and Economic Performance*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.

Distinguishes institutions (the rules) from organisations (the players), and argues that institutional change is path-dependent and slow because *informal* norms — the shared mental models that give formal rules their legitimacy — change far more slowly than the formal rules themselves. This is the cleanest pre-existing articulation of an L₅-bound floor that does not assume biological generation. **Bears on:** H₀; M-Conjecture (technology may revise formal rules quickly but cannot revise informal-norm acculturation quickly).

### B. Organisational ecology and succession

**[4] Stinchcombe, Arthur L. 1965.** "Social Structure and Organizations." In James G. March (ed.), *Handbook of Organizations*. Chicago: Rand McNally, 142–193.

Establishes the *liability of newness*: young organisations have higher mortality rates because they lack stable role structures and external legitimacy. Stinchcombe also documents an "imprinting" finding: founding-era social conditions persist in organisations across decades through their embedded role structures. The imprinting result is the empirical ancestor of the M-Conjecture's claim that legitimacy-transfer is slower than channel/store revision. **Bears on:** H₀ definition of L₅; M-Conjecture (legitimacy-transfer process specifically resists update).

**[5] Hannan, Michael T., and John Freeman. 1977.** "The Population Ecology of Organizations." *American Journal of Sociology* 82(5): 929–964.

Founds organisational ecology. Argues that organisations face structural inertia and that change occurs primarily through population-level selection (births and deaths of organisations) rather than within-organisation adaptation. The structural-inertia thesis predicts that observed reform-cycle distributions in long-lived institutions will look stationary because the rapidly-changing organisations are systematically *removed from the archive* — a survivorship-bias argument. **Bears on:** H₀; R3 (survivorship bias); TC-08 (defunct-archive feasibility).

**[6] Hannan, Michael T., and John Freeman. 1989.** *Organizational Ecology*. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press.

Book-length synthesis of the 1977 framework with two decades of empirical work added (newspaper publishers, labour unions, semiconductor firms). Quantifies age- and size-dependent mortality. The age-dependent mortality findings are directly relevant to TC-08 defunct-archive interpretation: the survivorship asymmetry is not random but structured. **Bears on:** R3; TC-08.

**[7] Carroll, Glenn R., and Michael T. Hannan. 2000.** *The Demography of Corporations and Industries*. Princeton: Princeton University Press.

The synthesis text on organisational demography. Provides the methodological vocabulary — vital rates, density dependence, niche width — that TC-04 (T10 archive characterisation) can adopt for layered cycle analysis. Particularly relevant to the technical-versus-governance event-layer separation in the T10 schema. **Bears on:** TC-04 methodology.

**[8] Baron, James N., and Michael T. Hannan. 2002.** "Organizational Blueprints for Success in High-Tech Start-Ups: Lessons from the Stanford Project on Emerging Companies." *California Management Review* 44(3): 8–36.

The Stanford Project on Emerging Companies (SPEC) longitudinal study of ~200 Silicon Valley start-ups. Founders' early employment-model choices — five identifiable types — persist for years and influence organisational performance and survival. Companies were materially more likely to fail when they later altered the founder's blueprint. This is empirical evidence for *imprinting at the legitimacy layer* (the founder's authority pattern propagates), and supports the M-Conjecture claim that legitimacy-pattern revision is destabilising at any speed, not merely slow. **Bears on:** H₀; M-Conjecture; T1 (Bayer/Humanocracy as predicted instance: rapid blueprint change is destabilising).

### C. CEO and executive tenure

**[9] Hambrick, Donald C., and Phyllis A. Mason. 1984.** "Upper Echelons: The Organization as a Reflection of Its Top Managers." *Academy of Management Review* 9(2): 193–206.

The founding upper-echelons paper: organisational outcomes are partly predicted by top-management characteristics, including tenure. Establishes tenure as a measurable, theory-relevant variable rather than a demographic accident. **Bears on:** M2 (tenure series methodology).

**[10] Henderson, Andrew D., Danny Miller, and Donald C. Hambrick. 2006.** "How quickly do CEOs become obsolete? Industry dynamism, CEO tenure, and company performance." *Strategic Management Journal* 27(5): 447–460.

Empirical study of CEO obsolescence across stable and dynamic industries. Finds that the value of CEO tenure to firm performance varies sharply with industry dynamism — in dynamic industries, performance peaks early and decays. Important precisely because it shows that *external rate of change* (a candidate proxy for H₁ pressure) interacts with internal tenure-driven sclerosis. **Bears on:** H₁ (pressure interacts with tenure-stratified institutional capacity); M2.

**[11] Kaplan, Steven N., and Bernadette A. Minton. 2012.** "How Has CEO Turnover Changed?" *International Review of Finance* 12(1): 57–87.

Documents the rise in CEO turnover among large US firms 1992–2007, including increased performance-sensitivity of board-driven turnover. Average CEO tenure under 7 years over the sample; total turnover rate 15.8% per annum. Useful as the contemporary empirical anchor that M2 distribution analysis (Architect M2) needs: the *means* shifted, but the *variance and tail behaviour* are the H₁ signature. Re-analysis of this dataset under variance/kurtosis lenses is a candidate TC-09 supplementary task. **Bears on:** H₁ (second-order signatures); M2.

**[12] Spencer Stuart. 2025.** *2025 S&P 1500 CEO Transitions: Behind the CEO Moment*. Practitioner research report.

Contemporary data series. 168 new S&P 1500 CEO appointments in 2025 (highest since 2010); 84% first-time enterprise CEOs; average incoming-CEO age 54.4 years (down from 55.8 the year before); average CEO tenure 8.5 years (down from 9.1 in 2021). Practitioner sources are not peer-reviewed and require Verifier scrutiny on methodology, but they carry recent data that academic literature does not. **Bears on:** H₁ (turnover acceleration as second-order pressure signature); M2 contemporary data anchor. **Verifier note:** confirm methodology section of the report; check whether the tenure decline survives controls for industry mix, M&A activity, and pandemic-era extraordinary turnover.

### D. Legislator and political tenure

**[13] Polsby, Nelson W. 1968.** "The Institutionalization of the U.S. House of Representatives." *American Political Science Review* 62(1): 144–168.

Documents the long-run rise of mean tenure and seniority-based norms in the US House across the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. The institutionalisation thesis — that long-tenured legislators with internalised procedural norms are the substrate of stable rule-bound governance — is the cleanest legislative-domain analogue of L₅ legitimacy-transfer. Subsequent work (Squire and others) has extended the institutionalisation analysis to state legislatures and to comparative parliaments; the Verifier pass should add at least one comparative-parliament source for v0.2. **Bears on:** H₀ (legislative-domain L₅ anchor); contemporary tenure-decline literature should be read against this baseline.

### E. Cohort replacement in scientific fields

**[14] Hull, David L. 1988.** *Science as a Process: An Evolutionary Account of the Social and Conceptual Development of Science*. Chicago: University of Chicago Press.

Detailed historical-philosophical study of cohort succession in numerical taxonomy and cladistics, arguing that scientific change proceeds through demic competition between research groups whose cohort composition changes over decades. Hull's account is granular at the legitimacy-transfer-process level: succession of authoritative judgement passes through specific apprenticeship and credential lineages, not through generic information transfer. This is the philosophical-of-science complement to the M-Conjecture: technology can move the *content* of scientific judgement quickly while the *legitimacy of judgement-makers* moves at cohort speed. **Bears on:** M-Conjecture; M3 (custodial-attenuation in non-monastic, non-artisanal traditions).

**[15] Azoulay, Pierre, Christian Fons-Rosen, and Joshua S. Graff Zivin. 2019.** "Does Science Advance One Funeral at a Time?" *American Economic Review* 109(8): 2889–2920.

Empirical test of Planck's principle. Following premature deaths of star life scientists, contributions by their collaborators decline while contributions by *non-collaborators* increase markedly, drawing on different prior literatures and disproportionately producing high-impact work. The paper documents that legitimacy-bound gating of a field — the star scientist's authority to recognise valid contribution — slows substitution of intellectual cohort even when the underlying technology of communication is fast. This is the cleanest *causal* empirical evidence in the cited literature for the M-Conjecture: the rate-limiting step is legitimacy of recognition, not channel speed or knowledge availability. **Bears on:** M-Conjecture (primary empirical anchor outside the institutional-history literature); H₀ (legitimacy-transfer in scientific fields appears L₅-paced even where formal cohort replacement is faster).

## 4. Coverage assessment

The v0.1 fill addresses the four target branches identified by the Architect bibliography gap:

- **Organisational ecology / succession** — covered by entries [4]–[8]. Coverage is canonical; contemporary post-2015 empirical work is under-represented.
- **CEO and executive tenure** — covered by entries [9]–[12], including one practitioner data point. Coverage is solid but US-centric.
- **Legislator tenure** — covered minimally by entry [13]. Single source; v0.2 of this fill should add comparative-parliament work and at least one source on European parliaments.
- **Cohort replacement in scientific fields** — covered by entries [14]–[15]. Two sources is sparse; v0.2 should add at least one source on cohort effects in mathematics or theoretical physics, where succession patterns are documented and differ from the life-sciences pattern of [15].

Two cross-cutting gaps the Verifier should flag:

- **Religious-order succession** is uncovered. Given that TC-01 is the M1 pilot on Catholic ecumenical councils, the absence of contemporary academic literature on religious-order generational succession is a real coverage gap that the Verifier should treat as binding for v0.2.
- **AI-mediated coordination disruption** literature (relevant to FH-005, the M-Conjecture expiry-clock) is absent. Some of this literature is too recent to have a stable canon, but the Verifier should add at least two post-2023 sources on AI-mediated authority or AI-augmented decision-making in institutional settings.
