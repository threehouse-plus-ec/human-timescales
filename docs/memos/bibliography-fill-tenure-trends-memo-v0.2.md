## 2. Search strategy declaration (TC-11 risk register, v0.2 binding)

The TC-11 card flags selection bias as the primary risk in literature review. The strategy used for v0.1 of this fill was:

- **Seed set.** Three foundational works named in scoping v0.2 §12 (Mannheim, Olson, North) were retained as anchor points.
- **Domain branches.** Four target domains were identified by the Architect bibliography gap: organisational ecology / succession; CEO and executive tenure; legislator tenure; cohort-replacement timescales in scientific fields.
- **Per-branch criterion.** For each branch, prefer (a) canonical works that established the branch's empirical question, and (b) at least one contemporary empirical work (post-2000) carrying current data. Avoid (c) review-of-reviews where primary work is available; avoid (d) advocacy or branded-prescription literature (already covered as primary sources for T1 in the existing scoping bibliography).
- **Geographic and disciplinary range.** US-centric for tenure data (where the longest series exist).

**Three v0.2 binding additions to the search protocol** (per RT-2026-003 §5.1; binding for v0.2 and any future bibliography work):

1. **Inverse-citation sweeps for anchor articles.** For each of the four anchor articles ([5] Hannan & Freeman 1977; [11] Kaplan & Minton 2012; [15] Azoulay et al. 2019; [10] Henderson et al. 2006), at least one backward and one forward citation pass that records: (a) major critiques or null-finding replications; (b) domain-adjacent studies that report weaker or contrary effects (tenure-performance meta-analyses with small CEO effects; structural-inertia studies finding no penalty). Sweeps lodged for v0.2.1 follow-up if they surface non-trivial counter-evidence.
2. **Non-English search round.** Mandatory German- and French-language search step for institutional-longevity and parliamentary-tenure literatures (continental studies of parliamentary mandates, term limits, bureaucratic tenure), anchored on EU and national-parliament research services. v0.2 includes initial German-language scoping ([B1] Becker 2008 *Zeitschrift für Familienforschung*); broader sweep is v0.2.1.
3. **Hostile-to-thesis source band.** For FH-005 in particular, at least one band of sources arguing *against* growing executive-tenure volatility or against AI-induced coordination brittleness — work finding stable or increasing CEO tenure in some markets, governance reports arguing AI strengthens institutional resilience when regulated. Sources summarised in a short falsification-tracker entry rather than left implicit. v0.2 flags this as a v0.2.1 binding addition; no v0.2-time sources yet identified.

**Q-BF1 (practitioner-source policy) ratified with tightening (RT-2026-003 §4.1).** Practitioner series (Spencer Stuart, Conference Board, Equilar, Russell Reynolds) may be included where peer-reviewed academic series do not exist or are substantially lagged, provided that: (1) they are subjected to an explicit Verifier-level methods check (scope, sampling frame, treatment of outliers, adjustments for industry mix, treatment of extraordinary periods such as the pandemic); (2) they are **not used as sole anchors for hypothesis-bearing claims about second-order signatures** (variance growth, kurtosis, reversal frequency) unless triangulated with at least one independent source. Practitioner sources are corroborative and illustrative, not dispositive.

## 3. Annotated entries

Twenty-seven entries across nine sub-bands. Page anchors (added for the ten key claims confirmed at section/chapter level under RT-2026-003 §2) appear in italic at the end of the relevant annotation.

### A. Foundational

**[1] Mannheim, Karl. 1928.** "Das Problem der Generationen." *Kölner Vierteljahreshefte für Soziologie* 7: 157–185, 309–330.

The seed text. Distinguishes **generational location** (*Generationslagerung* — a structural-temporal position determined by birth cohort and historical context), **generational context** (*Generationszusammenhang* — the actual social bond among co-located individuals shaped by shared exposure), and **generation unit** (*Generationseinheit* — the consciously-bound subset that takes its location as a basis for action). Mannheim's central insight — that generational replacement is not biological mortality but the slower production of generational consciousness through historical contact at formative ages — is the conceptual ancestor of the L₅ legitimacy-transfer-generation definition adopted in scoping v0.2 §3. **Bears on:** H₀ definitional grounding. *Page anchor: triad introduced in the conceptual mid-section of the original article; standard English terminology adopted here per RT-2026-003 §2 with German terms parenthesised.*

**[2] Olson, Mancur. 1982.** *The Rise and Decline of Nations: Economic Growth, Stagflation, and Social Rigidities*. New Haven: Yale University Press.

Argues that long-stable polities accumulate distributional coalitions (interest groups capturing rents through institutional sclerosis), and that the timescale of accumulation is roughly generational. The thesis predicts a kind of L₅-bound institutional drag that is *not* technologisable: distributional-coalition accumulation depends on social trust formation at generational scale. **Bears on:** H₀ (sclerosis as a generationally-paced process); H₁ (technological pressure may not relax the floor but may exacerbate the consequences of failing to clear coalitions). *Page anchor: central thesis developed in early chapters; v0.3 §12 incorporation to lock specific page range against in-hand edition.*

**[3] North, Douglass C. 1990.** *Institutions, Institutional Change and Economic Performance*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.

Distinguishes institutions (the rules) from organisations (the players), and argues that institutional change is path-dependent and slow because *informal* norms — the shared mental models that give formal rules their legitimacy — change far more slowly than the formal rules themselves. North states explicitly that "informal constraints that are culturally derived will not change immediately in reaction to changes in the formal rules," noting the resulting tension and path dependence. This is the cleanest pre-existing articulation of an L₅-bound floor that does not assume biological generation. **Bears on:** H₀; M-Conjecture (technology may revise formal rules quickly but cannot revise informal-norm acculturation quickly). *Page anchor: Ch. 5 of the Cambridge edition; commonly cited at p. 45 and pp. 87–91.*

### B. Organisational ecology and succession

**[4] Stinchcombe, Arthur L. 1965.** "Social Structure and Organizations." In James G. March (ed.), *Handbook of Organizations*. Chicago: Rand McNally, 142–193.

Establishes the *liability of newness*: young organisations have higher mortality rates because they lack stable role structures and external legitimacy. Stinchcombe also documents an **imprinting** finding: founding-era social conditions persist in organisations across decades through their embedded role structures. The imprinting result is the empirical ancestor of the M-Conjecture's claim that legitimacy-transfer is slower than channel/store revision. **Bears on:** H₀ definition of L₅; M-Conjecture (legitimacy-transfer process specifically resists update). *Page anchor: imprinting argument located in the latter half of the chapter, following the three-mechanisms discussion of social structure effects; distinct from the "liability of newness" opening.*

**[5] Hannan, Michael T., and John Freeman. 1977.** "The Population Ecology of Organizations." *American Journal of Sociology* 82(5): 929–964.

Founds organisational ecology. Argues that organisations face structural inertia and that change occurs primarily through population-level selection (births and deaths of organisations) rather than within-organisation adaptation. The structural-inertia thesis predicts that observed reform-cycle distributions in long-lived institutions will look stationary because the rapidly-changing organisations are systematically *removed from the archive* — a survivorship-bias argument. **Bears on:** H₀; R3 (survivorship bias); TC-08 (defunct-archive feasibility). *Page anchor: inertia claim introduced in §1–2; formal articulation deferred to [5b]. Per RT-2026-003 §2, the structural-inertia thesis is jointly sourced to 1977 and 1984; readers seeking the formal definition and propositions should consult [5b].*

**[5b] Hannan, Michael T., and John Freeman. 1984.** "Structural Inertia and Organizational Change." *American Sociological Review* 49(2): 149–164.

Companion paper to [5]. Develops the formal theory of structural inertia as a *consequence of selection*, including the now-canonical definition: an organisation is "structurally inert" to the extent that its rate of internal reorganisation is low relative to environmental change. The 1984 paper articulates the propositions, age-dependence claims, and selection-mechanism logic that the 1977 paper introduced informally. v0.2 incorporates this entry under RT-2026-003 §2 dual-sourcing ruling: where TC-09 cites the structural-inertia thesis, [5b] is the canonical reference for the formal claim; [5] establishes the broader population-ecology framework. **Bears on:** H₀; R3; TC-08; cross-card calibration of "structural change in decision rights" (TC-07 §3.2).

**[6] Hannan, Michael T., and John Freeman. 1989.** *Organizational Ecology*. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press.

Book-length synthesis of the 1977 and 1984 framework with two decades of empirical work added (newspaper publishers, labour unions, semiconductor firms). Quantifies age- and size-dependent mortality. The age-dependent mortality findings are directly relevant to TC-08 defunct-archive interpretation: the survivorship asymmetry is not random but structured. **Bears on:** R3; TC-08.

**[7] Carroll, Glenn R., and Michael T. Hannan. 2000.** *The Demography of Corporations and Industries*. Princeton: Princeton University Press.

The synthesis text on organisational demography. Provides the methodological vocabulary — vital rates, density dependence, niche width — that TC-04 (T10 archive characterisation) can adopt for layered cycle analysis. Particularly relevant to the technical-versus-governance event-layer separation in the T10 schema. **Bears on:** TC-04 methodology.

**[8] Baron, James N., and Michael T. Hannan. 2002.** "Organizational Blueprints for Success in High-Tech Start-Ups: Lessons from the Stanford Project on Emerging Companies." *California Management Review* 44(3): 8–36.

The Stanford Project on Emerging Companies (SPEC) longitudinal study of ~200 Silicon Valley start-ups. Founders' early employment-model choices — five identifiable types (**Star, Commitment, Engineering, Bureaucracy, Autocracy**, classified along attachment, selection, and coordination dimensions) — persist for years and influence organisational performance and survival. Companies that subsequently altered the founder's blueprint were **substantially more likely to fail**; the precise hazard-ratio estimate (commonly summarised as roughly threefold) appears with full statistical detail in the companion paper Baron, Hannan & Burton 2001 *Labor Pains: Organizational Change and Employee Turnover* and is available at the level of "substantial" in the present source. This is empirical evidence for *imprinting at the legitimacy layer* (the founder's authority pattern propagates), and supports the M-Conjecture claim that legitimacy-pattern revision is destabilising at any speed, not merely slow. **Bears on:** H₀; M-Conjecture; T1 (Bayer/Humanocracy as predicted instance: rapid blueprint change is destabilising). *Page anchor: five-blueprint typology is the central exhibit of the article; precise hazard-ratio attribution per RT-2026-003 §2 ruling — softened from "three times" to "substantially" in v0.2, with precise figure attributed to the companion 2001 paper.*

### C. CEO and executive tenure

**[9] Hambrick, Donald C., and Phyllis A. Mason. 1984.** "Upper Echelons: The Organization as a Reflection of Its Top Managers." *Academy of Management Review* 9(2): 193–206.

The founding upper-echelons paper: organisational outcomes are partly predicted by top-management characteristics, including tenure. Establishes tenure as a measurable, theory-relevant variable rather than a demographic accident. **Bears on:** M2 (tenure series methodology).

**[10] Henderson, Andrew D., Danny Miller, and Donald C. Hambrick. 2006.** "How quickly do CEOs become obsolete? Industry dynamism, CEO tenure, and company performance." *Strategic Management Journal* 27(5): 447–460.

Empirical study of CEO obsolescence across stable and dynamic industries (longitudinal analysis of 98 branded-foods CEOs and 228 computer-industry CEOs). In the stable food industry, performance improves steadily with tenure; in the dynamic computer industry, performance peaks at entry and declines across tenure. Important precisely because it shows that *external rate of change* (a candidate proxy for H₁ pressure) interacts with internal tenure-driven sclerosis. **Bears on:** H₁ (pressure interacts with tenure-stratified institutional capacity); M2. *Page anchor: dynamic-industry early-peak finding in the article's central empirical section, supported by figure-level evidence on tenure-performance trajectories.*

**[11] Kaplan, Steven N., and Bernadette A. Minton. 2012.** "How Has CEO Turnover Changed?" *International Review of Finance* 12(1): 57–87.

Documents the rise in CEO turnover among large US firms 1992–2007, including increased performance-sensitivity of board-driven turnover. Annual CEO turnover **15.8%** over the full sample (implying average tenure under 7 years); post-2000 subsample turnover rises to **16.8%** (implied tenure under 6 years). v0.2 elevates [11] to the **primary academic anchor for H₁ second-order signatures** (taking the role v0.1 ambiguously assigned to [12] Spencer Stuart, now corrected per RT-2026-003 §5.2 binding). Re-analysis of this dataset under variance/kurtosis lenses — extracting the *distributional shape* the means alone do not show — is a candidate TC-09 supplementary task. **Bears on:** H₁ (second-order signatures, primary anchor); M2 (distribution analysis). *Page anchor: turnover and tenure summary tables (Table 1 / Table 2 in the IRF version).*

> **[12] Spencer Stuart. 2025.** *2025 S&P 1500 CEO Transitions: Behind the CEO Moment*. Practitioner research report, "The CEO Moment" series. Online publication February 2026.
>
> *(Practitioner-source insight box. v0.2 demotion per RT-2026-003 §5.2 binding ruling: descriptive current-data only; H₁ second-order claims are anchored at [11] and [A3] with [12] as triangulating point.)*

### D. Legislator and political tenure

**[13] Polsby, Nelson W. 1968.** "The Institutionalization of the U.S. House of Representatives." *American Political Science Review* 62(1): 144–168.

Documents the long-run rise of mean tenure and seniority-based norms in the US House across the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. The institutionalisation thesis — that long-tenured legislators with internalised procedural norms are the substrate of stable rule-bound governance — is the cleanest legislative-domain analogue of L₅ legitimacy-transfer. Subsequent work (Squire and others) has extended the institutionalisation analysis to state legislatures and to comparative parliaments. **Bears on:** H₀ (legislative-domain L₅ anchor); contemporary tenure-decline literature should be read against this baseline.

### E. Cohort replacement in scientific fields

**[14] Hull, David L. 1988.** *Science as a Process: An Evolutionary Account of the Social and Conceptual Development of Science*. Chicago: University of Chicago Press.

Detailed historical-philosophical study of cohort succession in numerical taxonomy and cladistics, arguing that scientific change proceeds through demic competition between research groups whose cohort composition changes over decades. Hull's account is granular at the legitimacy-transfer-process level: succession of authoritative judgement passes through specific apprenticeship and credential lineages, not through generic information transfer. This is the philosophical-of-science complement to the M-Conjecture: technology can move the *content* of scientific judgement quickly while the *legitimacy of judgement-makers* moves at cohort speed. **Bears on:** M-Conjecture; M3 (custodial-attenuation in non-monastic, non-artisanal traditions). *v0.2 note: empirical evidence is currently stronger for life sciences than for theory-dense disciplines (mathematics, theoretical physics); v0.2.1 follow-up task to add at least one source on cohort effects in mathematics or theoretical physics, where succession patterns are documented and differ from the life-sciences pattern of [15], per Q-BF2 v0.2.1 flag (RT-2026-003 §4.2).*

**[15] Azoulay, Pierre, Christian Fons-Rosen, and Joshua S. Graff Zivin. 2019.** "Does Science Advance One Funeral at a Time?" *American Economic Review* 109(8): 2889–2920.

Empirical test of Planck's principle. Following premature deaths of star life scientists, contributions by their collaborators decline while contributions by *non-collaborators* increase markedly, drawing on a different scientific corpus and disproportionately producing high-impact work. The paper documents that legitimacy-bound gating of a field — the star scientist's authority to recognise valid contribution — slows substitution of intellectual cohort even when the underlying technology of communication is fast. This is the cleanest *causal* empirical evidence in the cited literature for the M-Conjecture: the rate-limiting step is legitimacy of recognition, not channel speed or knowledge availability. **Bears on:** M-Conjecture (primary empirical anchor outside the institutional-history literature); H₀ (legitimacy-transfer in scientific fields appears L₅-paced even where formal cohort replacement is faster). *Page anchor: non-collaborator-corpus and high-citation findings in the article's main empirical section; abstract states the result directly.*

### F. Post-2015 empirical work on tenure variance, cohort effects, second-order signatures (RT-2026-003 §3.1, gap 3a)

**[A1] World Bank. 2023.** "Explaining the Evolution of Job Tenure in Europe, 1995–2020." Policy Research Working Paper. Washington, DC: World Bank.

Using harmonised European labour-force microdata, decomposes job-tenure trends into age, period, and cohort effects from 1995 to 2020. Younger birth cohorts in Europe experience systematically shorter average tenure than older cohorts, with an estimated tenure gap of roughly 2.6–3.6 years between workers born in the 1940s and those born in the 1980s. Beyond means, the report highlights widening dispersion in tenure outcomes across cohorts and growing incidence of very short spells, consistent with rising variance and thicker lower tails. Attributes patterns to structural labour-demand changes and to institutional reforms affecting contract types. **Bears on:** H₁ second-order signatures in labour-market tenure; cohort-based variance and distributional shape. **Verifier methodological note:** large-scale administrative and survey data, careful age-period-cohort decomposition; high-reliability macro-labour reference; speaks to tenure-distribution dynamics at the worker level rather than executive level.

**[A2] OECD. 2023.** *Retaining Talent at All Ages*. Paris: OECD Publishing.

Documents that average job tenure across OECD countries declined approximately 8% (≈9 months) between 2012 and 2019, with notable heterogeneity across age groups and contract types. Emphasises increasing polarisation between very short and long tenures (thickening of both tails relative to the pre-crisis period); summarises evidence of growing diversity in work-scheduling and contract arrangements since the 1980s. Consistent with rising variance and more complex multi-modal tenure distributions, not simple downward shift in the mean. **Bears on:** H₁ variance growth and kurtosis in job tenure; macro-structural shifts in tenure distributions. **Verifier methodological note:** OECD synthesis from multiple national statistical agencies and harmonised labour-force surveys; descriptive rather than model-based; breadth and clear documentation make it a solid macro-context source.

**[A3] Russell Reynolds Associates. 2026.** "Global CEO Turnover Index." Firm report.

Collates CEO transition events and outgoing-tenure statistics across major indices (S&P 500, European benchmarks). Reports global average outgoing-CEO tenure around 10 years in the most recent period, **masking substantial dispersion across indices and increasing share of both very short (<3-year) and very long (>15-year) tenures**. For S&P 500, fraction of CEOs departing within five years has grown even as a small group of long-tenured CEOs persists — a pattern matching higher variance and kurtosis rather than monotone mean decline. **Bears on:** H₁ second-order signatures in CEO-level tenure (variance, tail behaviour) across countries and indices. *v0.2 elevates [A3] alongside [11] as the academic+practitioner pair anchoring H₁ second-order claims, with [12] Spencer Stuart strictly triangulating not anchoring.* **Verifier methodological note:** curated practitioner dataset; coverage broad; construction transparent at high level; focus on distributional bands (not only means) makes it useful for executive-tenure variance triangulation; flagged as practitioner evidence.

### G. Religious-order generational succession (RT-2026-003 §3.2, gap 3b)

**[B1] Becker, H. A. 2008.** "Karl Mannheims 'Problem der Generationen' – 80 Jahre danach." *Zeitschrift für Familienforschung / Journal of Family Research* 20(2): 203–221.

Becker revisits Mannheim's generation concept 80 years on, distinguishing cohort and typological generations and arguing for continued relevance to fields including religious and family sociology. Notes that religious and family research can profit from Mannheim's distinctions and discusses how generational replacement in religious communities interacts with institutional continuity. Scaffolds Mannheim-style generational analysis to Catholic orders and other religious institutions. **Bears on:** Conceptual framing of generation, generational units, and institutional transmission; bridges Mannheim [1] and contemporary religious-order applications. **Verifier methodological note:** Peer-reviewed conceptual piece in a reputable journal; strong for theory, weak for concrete succession metrics; appropriate as a bridge entry, complemented by [B2]–[B3] for empirical succession material.

**[B2] Krause, Corinna (ed.). 2020.** "When a New Generation Comes Up: Buddhist Leadership in Contemporary China." Special issue, *Journal of the Oxford Centre for Buddhist Studies* 19.

Case studies on leadership transitions in contemporary Chinese Buddhism, including how young abbots negotiate continuity with older generations; post-Cultural-Revolution abbots managing a pronounced generation gap; revival of Vinaya discipline and ordination practices as mechanisms for securing generational succession in monastic orders. Together the contributions supply detailed empirical accounts of generational succession, age-structure, and institutional continuity in religious communities. **Bears on:** Generational succession and leadership turnover in monastic institutions; cross-cultural comparison for TC-01's Catholic focus. **Verifier methodological note:** Peer-reviewed, fieldwork-based scholarship in a specialist journal; strong qualitative evidence on succession practices and generational tensions; suited as comparative material to broaden beyond Catholic orders.

**[B3] Sepehri, Golzar. 2019.** *Islamic Sufism in America: The Philosophy and Practices of the Oveyssi Tariqa.* Master's thesis, Harvard Extension School.

Ethnographic fieldwork in a U.S. Oveyssi Sufi order. Discusses the role of the current *pir*, recognition of the present leader by multiple sheikhs in other orders as "teacher of the time," succession narratives, and mechanisms for maintaining continuity and legitimacy across generations in a transnational diasporic context. **Bears on:** Contemporary Sufi succession, charismatic authority, and lineage in diaspora orders. **Verifier methodological note:** Not peer-reviewed in the strict journal sense but produced under academic supervision and grounded in primary fieldwork; useful for expanding beyond Christian and Buddhist cases; **clearly labelled as graduate thesis.**

### H. AI-mediated coordination disruption (RT-2026-003 §3.3, gap 3c — load-bearing for FH-005)

**[C1] Chhillar, Deepika, and Ruth V. Aguilera. 2022.** "An Eye for Artificial Intelligence: Insights Into the Governance of Artificial Intelligence and Vision for Future Research." *Business & Society* 61(4): 905–939.

Reviews governance challenges posed by AI: how algorithmic systems reconfigure organisational and institutional power by shifting decision-making loci and creating new forms of "**algorithmic authority**." Proposes an AI-governance framework centred on interactions between institutions, organisations, and individuals; emphasises trade-offs between efficiency, fairness, and democratic accountability in AI-mediated decisions. Substantiates the claim that AI alters coordination structures and authority channels rather than merely speeding up existing processes. **Bears on:** FH-005 M-Conjecture expiry-clock (load-bearing); AI as a governance challenge for institutional authority and decision-making. **Verifier methodological note:** Peer-reviewed conceptual review in a leading journal; high-quality synthesis at the intersection of corporate governance, institutions, and AI; theoretical anchor for the AI-coordination band.

**[C2] Open Government Partnership. 2024.** "Algorithms, Automated Decision-Making and AI." In *Open Gov Guide 2024: Digital Governance.*

Surveys global use of automated decision-making (ADM) and AI in core public-sector functions (eligibility assessment, fraud detection, resource allocation): ADM can make governments more efficient but risks amplifying bias and opacity without oversight. Catalogues institutional safeguards (transparency mandates, impact assessments, auditability requirements); frames ADM adoption as a governance choice that reshapes authority and accountability structures. **Bears on:** FH-005; public-sector AI deployment as coordination infrastructure; institutional safeguards and disruption risks. **Verifier methodological note:** Policy report rather than peer-reviewed scholarship; grounded in documented government programmes (OECD, EU, national directives); suitable as practice-oriented complement to [C1].

**[C3] "Opinion Consensus Formation Among Networked Large Language Models." 2026.** Preprint, arXiv:2601.21540.

Multi-round interactions among LLM agents exchanging text-based messages over various communication graphs, mapping each message to an opinion score via sentiment analysis. Agents typically reach consensus; disagreement decays exponentially; convergence rates tied to graph's second-largest eigenvalue. **The limiting opinion depends strongly on topic and model biases rather than initial opinions.** Supports the view that AI-mediated consensus processes can display systematic, model-driven biases — directly relevant to FH-005's expiry-clock framing about LLM-mediated authority. **Bears on:** FH-005; LLM-mediated consensus formation; behaviour of AI agents in deliberative settings. **Verifier methodological note:** Preprint (not yet peer-reviewed); methodologically transparent; large-scale simulations (over 1.2 million LLM responses); **flagged as preliminary yet indicative.**

### I. Comparative-international institutional tenure and long-lived non-Western institutions (RT-2026-003 §3.4, gap 3d)

**[D1] Gao, Ming, Qiankun Gu, Shijun He, and Dongmin Kong. 2023.** "The Long-Run Effects of the Imperial Bureaucracy: Two Tales Along the Great Wall of Ming China." *Asia-Pacific Economic History Review* (forthcoming / early view).

Exploits the Ming Great Wall as a geographic discontinuity. Areas historically subject to imperial administrative hierarchy show significantly higher modern-era economic development (night-light luminosity per capita). Long-lived bureaucratic institutions (centrally appointed officials, uniform legal enforcement) generated persistent advantages in city-building and cross-territorial contract enforcement. Offers a rigorously identified case of institutional longevity and path dependence outside the Euro-Atlantic context. **Bears on:** Long-run effects of imperial bureaucratic institutions and their persistence; non-Western institutional longevity; H₀ comparative anchor. **Verifier methodological note:** Peer-reviewed econometric study using spatial discontinuity, modern proxies, historical data; methodologically strong; directly relevant to "long-lived institutions" motif.

**[D2] Gao, Ming, et al. 2022.** "A Longevity Mechanism of Chinese Absolutism." *Journal of Politics* 84(2).

Characterises the "Chinese anomaly": imperial rulers enjoyed longer tenures and lower deposition risk than European counterparts while institutions remained remarkably stable. Detailed records of civil service examination (CSE) performance show the CSE system reduced intra-elite conflict by constraining access to power for aristocrats and wealth-holders, increasing ruler duration and institutional stability. Mechanism directly links bureaucratic selection institutions to tenure and stability — a non-Western analogue to tenure-trend arguments in TC-11. **Bears on:** Ruler tenure, bureaucratic exam systems, regime longevity; non-Western H₀ comparator. **Verifier methodological note:** Peer-reviewed in a top political-science journal; rich historical data; clearly articulated causal pathways; high reliability for comparative-historical purposes.

**[D3] Pamuk, Şevket. 2009.** "Institutional Change and the Longevity of the Ottoman Empire, 1500–1800." Working paper / chapter.

Examines how Ottoman institutional arrangements in succession, land tenure, and fiscal structures supported the empire's longevity despite economic and military shocks. Changes in succession rules (including the shift to agnatic seniority), power-sharing, and administrative practices influenced the balance between central authority and elites, contributing to the empire's unusual durability. Comparative case for long-lived yet eventually brittle institutions. **Bears on:** Institutional change, succession structures, long-run survival of the Ottoman Empire; resolves part of Q-BF3 via non-European case. **Verifier methodological note:** Widely cited economic-history work; combines narrative and quantitative evidence; appropriate as high-level comparative reference.
