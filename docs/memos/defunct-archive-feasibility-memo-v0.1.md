## 1. Purpose

Scoping v0.2 R3 (Scout signal 2): the institutional archive that founds TC-01 consists precisely of institutions that survived. If failed institutions had systematically shorter reform cycles — oscillated themselves to death, in the H₁ signature — observed survivor-stationarity is a selection effect, not a finding about underlying L₅ rates. The structural answer is to attempt reform-cycle archaeology on at least one *defunct* institutional archive and check whether the survivor pattern holds.

This memo executes the TC-08 feasibility check. Its question is not "what was the reform pattern of defunct institution X" but the prior question: **can the reform pattern of any defunct institution be recovered at the resolution that M1 requires?** If yes, with named caveats: feasibility confirmed and TC-01 should be paired with a defunct-archive companion run. If no, with reasoned cause: feasibility declined, R3 acknowledged but not pretended-away.

The TC-08 success criterion (per task card) is a binary verdict with reasoning. This memo issues such a verdict.

---

## 2. Feasibility criteria

For a defunct-institution archive to be usable for M1 method work, the following must hold:

1. **Lifespan-resolution match.** The institution's lifespan (foundation → dissolution) must be long enough that more than two or three reform events plausibly occurred within it. An institution that lasted twenty years, no matter how documented, gives at most one inter-event interval.
2. **Reform-event recoverability.** A non-trivial subset of the institution's reform events (per TC-07 inclusion test: documentary ratification + structural change in decision rights or membership rules + multi-domain-legitimacy applicability) must be recoverable from extant primary sources.
3. **Dating precision.** Recovered events must carry dating precision sufficient for inter-event interval extraction at TC-06's selected commensurability protocol resolution. Year-precision is the threshold; decade-precision is not.
4. **Survival asymmetry assessable.** The asymmetry of documentary survival — what kinds of documents preferentially survived dissolution, and what kinds were lost — must be characterisable. Without this characterisation, the recovered reform-event count cannot be honestly compared to TC-01's surviving-institution count.
5. **Dissolution generality.** The dissolution itself must be informative about institutional dynamics rather than reducible entirely to external political contingency. A purely externally-imposed dissolution (e.g., enemy conquest) tells us nothing about whether the institution would otherwise have continued reforming on an L₅-paced trajectory.

Conditions 1–3 are operational. Condition 4 is methodological discipline. Condition 5 is the substantive constraint that forces case selection.

## 3. Primary case: Knights Templar (1119–1312)

### 3.1 Lifespan and reform-event candidates

The Order of the Temple was founded c. 1119 in Jerusalem under Hugues de Payens and was suppressed in 1312 by Clement V's bull *Vox in excelso* at the Council of Vienne. Lifespan: ~193 years. Condition 1 (lifespan-resolution) is comfortably satisfied.

Candidate reform events, with TC-07 inclusion-test pass and dating:

| Event | Date | Documentary ratification | Sub-type tags |
|---|---|---|---|
| Council of Troyes — Latin Rule (Regula primitiva) adopted | 1129 | Council acta; Rule text via Curzon (1886) edition | structural, disciplinary |
| Bull *Omne datum optimum* (Innocent II) — Order made autonomous from local episcopate | 1139 | Vatican Bullarium | structural |
| Bull *Milites Templi* (Celestine II) — alms-collection privileges | 1144 | Vatican Bullarium | disciplinary |
| Bull *Militia Dei* (Eugene III) — own oratories permitted | 1145 | Vatican Bullarium | structural |
| Hierarchical Statutes — under Bertrand de Blanquefort (Grand Master 1156–1169) | c. 1156–1169 | Curzon (1886) Règle du Temple | structural, disciplinary |
| French Statutes (Retrais) | c. 1230s–1260s | Curzon (1886) | structural, disciplinary |
| "Customs" (Égards) and later additions | c. 1260s–1290s | Curzon (1886) | disciplinary |
| Bull *Pastoralis praeeminentiae* (Clement V) — universal arrest order | 22 November 1307 | Vatican Bullarium | structural (arrest) |
| Bull *Vox in excelso* — formal suppression | 22 March 1312, approved 3 April 1312 | Vatican Bullarium; Council of Vienne acta | structural (dissolution) |
| Bulls *Ad providam* (2 May 1312) and *Nuper in concilio* (16 May 1312) — disposition of property | 1312 | Vatican Bullarium | structural |

Seven to eight events with year-precision dates over 193 years; one cluster (Hierarchical Statutes) and one cluster (French Statutes / Customs) bracket-dated to a decade or so.

**Inclusion test (§3 of TC-07).** The four pre-suppression papal bulls (1139, 1144, 1145) and the Council of Troyes (1129) all pass: documentary ratification (Vatican or council documents), structural or disciplinary change in decision rights, multi-domain-legitimacy applicability via papal ratification. The Hierarchical Statutes and French Statutes pass on documentary-survival grounds but with degraded dating precision. The 1307–1312 cluster is unambiguous.

**Per-century event density.** Scoping v0.2 anchors L₅ at ~25–40 years between reform events. Templar event spacing under year-precision dates: 1129 → 1139 (10 yr), 1139 → 1144 (5 yr), 1144 → 1145 (1 yr), 1145 → ~1165 (~20 yr), ~1165 → ~1240 (~75 yr), ~1240 → 1307 (~67 yr). The mean of the year-precision-only intervals is ~12 years; including bracket-dated events extends to ~25 years. **First-pass observation:** Templars exhibit a *front-loaded* reform pattern (intense first 30 years, slow thereafter), which is itself an L₅-relevant datum if it generalises.

### 3.2 Documentary survival profile

Templar primary sources fall into three classes with sharply different survival:

- **Papal-side documents.** Vatican Archives retained papal bulls, council acta, and trial-related papal correspondence. Survival rate: very high.
- **Internal Templar documents.** Chapter records, internal correspondence, Order's own copies of statutes. Survival rate: poor but not zero; Henri de Curzon's 1886 *La Règle du Temple* (Société de l'histoire de France edition) reconstructed the corpus from discoveries made in Rome, Dijon, and Paris between 1794 and 1840.
- **Royal-administration documents.** French royal archives (Philip IV's administration) retained extensive trial records 1307–1312, voluminous and politically motivated.

The asymmetry is severe and matters: the Templars' externally-ratified events (papal bulls) survive in the Vatican; their internally-ratified events (chapter decisions, custom adoption) survive only patchily through the Curzon reconstruction. Reform events at the institution-internal layer are systematically under-counted relative to the papal-ratified layer.

### 3.3 Caveats

- **Over-determination of dissolution.** Templar suppression was not endogenous. Philip IV of France owed the Order substantial sums; his administration drove the political case; Clement V was constrained by his Avignonese position. The dissolution carries minimal information about whether the Templars would have otherwise continued reforming on an L₅ trajectory. Condition 5 (dissolution generality) is **partially failed**: the dissolution is informative about external-attack vulnerability, not about endogenous institutional dynamics.
- **Asymmetric documentary survival.** Per §3.2: papal-side bulls survive disproportionately. Any reform-event count drawn from the Templar archive will be biased toward externally-ratified events.
- **Doctrinal sub-type absent.** The Templars produced no doctrinal reforms in the TC-07 §5.1 sense. Their archive permits only structural and disciplinary sub-typing — a constraint on cross-case comparison with TC-01.

### 3.4 Verdict for Templars

**Feasibility confirmed with caveats.** Conditions 1, 2, 3 are satisfied; condition 4 is satisfied (the asymmetry is characterisable, as §3.2 has just done); condition 5 is partially failed in a way that the eventual TC-09 ledger entry must declare in writing. The Templar archive is usable for *structural and disciplinary* reform-cycle archaeology with explicit declaration of (a) the papal-versus-internal documentary asymmetry, (b) the doctrinal-sub-type absence, and (c) the over-determined character of the 1307–1312 dissolution.

The Templar archive is *not* usable as a stand-alone defunct-institution control. It must be paired with at least one secondary case to triangulate dissolution dynamics.

## 4. Secondary candidates considered

### 4.1 Society of Jesus suppression and restoration (1773–1814)

The Jesuits were suppressed by *Dominus ac Redemptor* (Clement XIV, 21 July 1773) and restored by *Sollicitudo omnium ecclesiarum* (Pius VII, 7 August 1814). Documentary survival is excellent on all sides (Vatican, suppressing monarchs' archives, Jesuit-internal records preserved in Russia and Prussia where the Order continued).

**Strengths.** Clean external and internal records; the Russia–Prussia continuity gives an unusual control on what the Order would have done absent suppression; the 41-year suppression period is itself a candidate L₅-scale interruption.

**Weaknesses.** *The Order was not in fact defunct.* It survived in the Russian Empire (with Catherine II's protection) and in Prussia, then expanded after 1814 into what is now its modern form. As a feasibility test for *truly defunct* institutional archives, the Jesuit case fails condition 5: dissolution did not in fact occur. The case is more useful as a *suppression-and-restoration* control on M-Conjecture (technology mediating restoration legitimacy) than as a survivorship-bias control on H₀.

**Verdict.** Not adopted as the secondary defunct-archive case, but flagged for separate consideration under TC-04 / M-Conjecture work.

### 4.2 English Dissolution monasteries (1536–1541), Glastonbury exemplar

Henry VIII's Dissolution of the Monasteries (1536–1541) abolished ~800 religious houses in England within five years. Each was a distinct institution with its own founding date, internal reform history (Benedictine, Cluniac, Cistercian, Augustinian, Carthusian variants), and dissolution date.

**Glastonbury Abbey** (founded c. 7th century, dissolved 15 November 1539 with the execution of Abbot Richard Whiting) is the largest and best-documented exemplar:

- **Lifespan.** ~900 years pre-dissolution; condition 1 trivially satisfied.
- **Reform-event recoverability.** Glastonbury underwent multiple reform episodes under Dunstan (10th century), the Anglo-Saxon reforms, post-Norman reorganisation, the 1184 fire and subsequent rebuilding, the Black Death period, late-medieval reforms. Each can be characterised at year-precision or decade-precision via standard medievalist scholarship.
- **Documentary survival.** Asymmetric. The Abbey library was renowned (John Leland documented unique manuscripts), but only ~40 manuscripts are known to survive. The trial records of Abbot Whiting and Cromwell's justification for his execution have *not survived* — even Cromwell's normally meticulous administrative record fails here. So the dissolution event itself is partially lost from the documentary record.
- **Dissolution generality.** The 1536–1541 Dissolution was externally-imposed and politically over-determined (Henry VIII's break with Rome, financial motive, Cromwell's administrative project). Like the Templar suppression, the dissolution carries minimal information about endogenous reform-cycle dynamics. Condition 5 partially failed.

**Cohort analysis option.** A unique strength of the Dissolution case: ~800 institutions with overlapping pre-1536 histories were dissolved within a five-year window. This permits **cohort-level survivorship-bias analysis**: which monastic houses had robust documentary survival (the larger, older, wealthier ones), which did not (smaller priories whose cartularies dispersed), and whether the documentary-survival pattern correlates with pre-dissolution reform-cycle pattern. This kind of analysis is unavailable for the singleton Templar case.

**Verdict.** Adopted as the secondary defunct-archive case, with **cohort-level analysis** preferred over Glastonbury-singleton analysis. Recommendation: extract pre-Dissolution reform-cycle data on a sample of 8–12 monastic houses with known documentary-survival profiles, paired with their Dissolution dispositions. The cohort approach mitigates the singleton-Glastonbury vulnerability to over-determination by averaging across houses with diverse pre-Dissolution trajectories.

### 4.3 Failed corporations (briefly)

Architect M3 in scoping v0.2 raised diversification across institutional traditions. Failed corporations — South Sea Company (1720 collapse), original East India Company (dissolved 1874), Standard Oil (broken up 1911) — have rich documentary records and clean dissolution events. However:

- **H₀ scope restriction.** Per scoping v0.2 §1, H₀ applies to multi-domain-legitimacy institutions. Commercial-legal-legitimacy bodies fall outside H₀ scope.
- **Use under M-Conjecture, not H₀.** Failed corporations may be useful as M-Conjecture controls (technology-vs-coordination-rate) but they are not the right test bed for the survivorship-bias control on H₀.

**Verdict.** Not adopted for TC-08. Flagged for possible TC-04 supplementary use under M-Conjecture work.

## 5. Verdict

**Feasibility confirmed for two paired defunct cases:**

1. **Knights Templar (1119–1312)** — primary, with explicit declaration of (a) papal-versus-internal documentary asymmetry, (b) doctrinal sub-type absence, (c) over-determined dissolution. Feasible at decade-precision for some events, year-precision for others; usable for structural and disciplinary reform-cycle archaeology only.
2. **English Dissolution cohort (1536–1541), 8–12 monastic houses sampled** — secondary, with cohort-level analysis to mitigate singleton-case over-determination. Feasible at year-precision for the dissolution event itself and decade-or-better precision for prior reform episodes.

The pairing is intentional. The Templar archive carries the survivorship-bias control proper (reform-cycle pattern of an institution that did not survive); the Dissolution cohort carries the cohort-level documentary-survival analysis (whether documentary survival itself correlates with reform pattern). Together they address R3 at both layers.

**Feasibility declined for:** Society of Jesus (not in fact defunct); failed corporations (out of H₀ scope).

## 6. Recommendations

1. **Adopt the paired-case approach.** TC-09 (Breakwater ledger entry) should treat the survivorship-bias control as confirmed feasible only on the paired-case basis. A singleton Templar analysis is insufficient given §3.3 caveats.
2. **Templar work should follow TC-07 sub-type tagging strictly.** Doctrinal-sub-type absence is itself a finding, not a coverage gap — note in writing.
3. **Dissolution-cohort sample selection** should be declared in writing before extraction, with selection criteria (size, order, region, documentary-survival profile) explicit. Random sampling is insufficient given the cohort's documentary-survival heterogeneity.
4. **Resource estimate.** Templar reform-cycle extraction: ~1 week of focused work given Curzon (1886), the Vatican Bullarium, and standard medievalist secondary literature (e.g., Malcolm Barber's *The New Knighthood*). Dissolution-cohort extraction: ~2–3 weeks given the per-house documentary work. Combined: ~3–4 weeks.
5. **Domain consultant.** Engagement of a medievalist or a historian of religious orders is recommended for both cases. Specifically, the Hierarchical Statutes dating window (1156–1169) and the French Statutes dating window (1230s–1260s) are sufficiently consequential to TC-09 inference that a specialist confirmation is worth seeking before extraction is committed.

## 7. Open questions

- **Q-DA1.** Whether the Templar-archive "front-loaded" reform pattern (intense first ~30 years, then slow) is generic to early-stage religious orders or is a case-specific Templar feature. A comparison with the early-stage Hospitaller (1099–) or early-stage Cistercian (1098–) reform-event spacings would settle this. Recommendation: include a brief comparison in the eventual TC-09 entry, even if not in TC-08 v0.1.
- **Q-DA2.** Whether the Dissolution-cohort sample should include the larger and richer houses (Glastonbury, Westminster, Bury St Edmunds) only, or should include smaller priories where documentary survival is poorer. The argument for inclusion-only-of-larger-houses: cleaner data, higher documentary survival. The argument for mixed inclusion: the survival asymmetry itself is what we want to characterise. Drafter recommendation: mixed, with explicit per-house survival-profile annotation.
- **Q-DA3.** Whether the Templar-cum-Dissolution paired analysis should be reported in TC-09 as a single bundled survivorship-bias result, or as two separately-classified findings. Drafter recommendation: separately-classified, given the different mechanisms of dissolution (suppression versus expropriation) and the different institutional types (military order versus monastic).
- **Q-DA4.** Whether the Curzon (1886) reconstruction has been superseded by more recent critical editions of the Templar Rule and statutes. The Verifier (or domain consultant) should check; if so, the more recent edition should be the working source.
