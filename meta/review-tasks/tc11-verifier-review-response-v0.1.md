### 3.1 Post-2015 empirical work on tenure variance, cohort effects, and second-order signatures (gap 3a)

**[A1] World Bank. 2023. "Explaining the Evolution of Job Tenure in Europe, 1995–2020." Policy Research Working Paper. Washington, DC: World Bank.**

- **Bears on:** H₁ second-order signatures in labour-market tenure; cohort-based variance and distributional shape.
- **Annotation:** Using harmonised European labour-force microdata, the authors decompose job-tenure trends into age, period, and cohort effects from 1995 to 2020. Younger birth cohorts experience systematically shorter average tenure than older cohorts, with an estimated tenure gap of roughly 2.6–3.6 years between workers born in the 1940s and those born in the 1980s. The report highlights widening dispersion in tenure outcomes and growing incidence of very short spells, consistent with rising variance and thicker lower tails.
- **Verifier methodological note:** Large-scale administrative and survey data, careful age-period-cohort decomposition. High-reliability macro-labour reference. Speaks to tenure-distribution dynamics at the worker level rather than executive level.

**[A2] OECD. 2023. *Retaining Talent at All Ages*. Paris: OECD Publishing.**

- **Bears on:** H₁ variance growth and kurtosis in job tenure; macro-structural shifts in tenure distributions.
- **Annotation:** Average job tenure across OECD countries declined ~8% (≈9 months) between 2012 and 2019, with notable heterogeneity across age groups and contract types. Increasing polarisation between very short and long tenures indicates a thickening of both tails; growing diversity in work-scheduling and contract arrangements since the 1980s. Consistent with rising variance and more complex multi-modal tenure distributions, not simple downward shift in the mean.
- **Verifier methodological note:** Synthesis from multiple national statistical agencies and harmonised labour-force surveys. Descriptive rather than model-based; breadth and clear documentation make it solid macro-context.

**[A3] Russell Reynolds Associates. 2026. "Global CEO Turnover Index." Firm report.**

- **Bears on:** H₁ second-order signatures in CEO-level tenure (variance, tail behaviour) across countries and indices.
- **Annotation:** Collates CEO transition events and outgoing-tenure statistics across major indices (S&P 500, European benchmarks). Global average outgoing-CEO tenure rose to ~10 years in the most recent period; this masks substantial dispersion across indices and an increasing share of both very short (<3-year) and very long (>15-year) tenures. For S&P 500, the fraction of CEOs departing within five years has grown even as a small group of long-tenured CEOs persists — a pattern matching higher variance and kurtosis rather than monotone mean decline.
- **Verifier methodological note:** Curated practitioner dataset. Coverage broad; construction transparent at high level; focus on distributional bands (not only means) makes it useful for executive-tenure variance triangulation. Should be flagged as practitioner evidence and triangulated against peer-reviewed work.

### 3.2 Religious-order generational succession (gap 3b)

**[B1] Becker, H. A. 2008. "Karl Mannheims 'Problem der Generationen' – 80 Jahre danach." *Zeitschrift für Familienforschung / Journal of Family Research* 20(2): 203–221.**

- **Annotation:** Becker revisits Mannheim's generation concept 80 years on, distinguishing cohort and typological generations and arguing for continued relevance. Scaffolds Mannheim-style generational analysis to Catholic orders and other religious institutions.

**[B2] Krause, Corinna (ed.). 2020. "When a New Generation Comes Up: Buddhist Leadership in Contemporary China." Special issue, *Journal of the Oxford Centre for Buddhist Studies* 19.**

- **Bears on:** Generational succession and leadership turnover in monastic institutions; cross-cultural comparison for TC-01's Catholic focus.
- **Annotation:** Case studies on leadership transitions in contemporary Chinese Buddhism, including how young abbots negotiate continuity with older generations; post-Cultural-Revolution abbots managing generation gap; revival of Vinaya discipline and ordination practices as mechanisms for securing generational succession in monastic orders.
- **Verifier methodological note:** Peer-reviewed, fieldwork-based scholarship. Strong qualitative evidence on succession practices and generational tensions. Suited as comparative material to broaden beyond Catholic orders.

**[B3] Sepehri, Golzar. 2019. *Islamic Sufism in America: The Philosophy and Practices of the Oveyssi Tariqa.* Master's thesis, Harvard Extension School.**

### 3.4 Comparative-international institutional tenure and long-lived non-Western institutions (gap 3d)

**[D1] Gao, Ming, Qiankun Gu, Shijun He, and Dongmin Kong. 2023. "The Long-Run Effects of the Imperial Bureaucracy: Two Tales Along the Great Wall of Ming China." *Asia-Pacific Economic History Review* (forthcoming / early view).**

- **Bears on:** Long-run effects of imperial bureaucratic institutions and their persistence; non-Western institutional longevity.
- **Annotation:** Exploits the Ming Great Wall as a geographic discontinuity. Areas historically subject to imperial administrative hierarchy show significantly higher modern-era economic development (night-light luminosity per capita). Long-lived bureaucratic institutions (centrally appointed officials, uniform legal enforcement) generated persistent advantages in city-building and cross-territorial contract enforcement.
- **Verifier methodological note:** Peer-reviewed econometric study using spatial discontinuity, modern proxies, historical data. Methodologically strong; directly relevant to the "long-lived institutions" motif.

**[D2] Gao, Ming, et al. 2022. "A Longevity Mechanism of Chinese Absolutism." *Journal of Politics* 84(2).**

- **Bears on:** Ruler tenure, bureaucratic exam systems, and regime longevity in imperial China.
- **Annotation:** Characterises the "Chinese anomaly": imperial rulers enjoyed longer tenures and lower deposition risk than European counterparts while institutions remained remarkably stable. Detailed records of civil service examination performance show the CSE system reduced intra-elite conflict by constraining access to power for aristocrats and wealth-holders, increasing ruler duration and institutional stability. Mechanism directly links bureaucratic selection institutions to tenure and stability.
- **Verifier methodological note:** Peer-reviewed in a top political-science journal; rich historical data; clearly articulated causal pathways. High reliability for comparative-historical purposes.

**[D3] Pamuk, Şevket. 2009. "Institutional Change and the Longevity of the Ottoman Empire, 1500–1800." Working paper / chapter.**

- **Bears on:** Institutional change, succession structures, long-run survival of the Ottoman Empire; resolves part of Q-BF3.
- **Annotation:** Examines how Ottoman institutional arrangements in succession, land tenure, and fiscal structures supported the empire's longevity despite economic and military shocks. Changes in succession rules (incl. shift to agnatic seniority), power-sharing, and administrative practices influenced the balance between central authority and elites. Comparative case for long-lived yet eventually brittle institutions.
- **Verifier methodological note:** Widely cited economic-history work; combines narrative and quantitative evidence. Appropriate as a high-level comparative reference on institutional longevity and succession beyond Europe and China.

## §4 Rulings on Q-BF1, Q-BF2, Q-BF3 (Q4)

### 4.1 Q-BF1 — practitioner-source policy

**Ruling:** Adopt the drafter's proposed policy with a modest tightening. Practitioner series (Spencer Stuart, Conference Board, Equilar, Russell Reynolds) may be included **where peer-reviewed academic series do not exist or are substantially lagged**, provided that:

1. They are subjected to an explicit Verifier-level methods check (scope, sampling frame, treatment of outliers, adjustments for industry mix, treatment of extraordinary periods such as the pandemic), and
2. They are **not used as sole anchors for hypothesis-bearing claims about second-order signatures** (e.g. variance growth, kurtosis, reversal frequency) unless triangulated with at least one independent source.

In the CEO-tenure domain, Kaplan & Minton's panel study and related academic work provide robust mean-tenure and turnover estimates. Practitioner series add timeliness and richer descriptive breakdowns but their proprietary methods and limited transparency mean they are **corroborative and illustrative**, not dispositive.

## §5 Selection-bias adequacy and Spencer Stuart [12] methodological scrutiny (Q5)

### 5.1 Selection-bias check on TC-11 search strategy

**Verdict: directionally sound but improvable along three axes.**

The memo's search strategy is empirically broad — classic organisational-ecology / upper-echelons / institutional-change work; post-1980 work on executive tenure and performance; practitioner series for current descriptive statistics. The §3 additions extend coverage to macro-labour tenure distributions, comparative-international institutional longevity, religious orders, and AI-mediated governance. This reduces, but does not eliminate, the risk of confirmation bias and Western-centric selection.

**Three explicit additions to the search protocol, recommended for v0.2 and beyond:**

1. **Inverse-citation sweeps for core anchors.** For each of a small set of anchor articles (Hannan & Freeman 1977; Kaplan & Minton 2012; Azoulay et al. 2019; Henderson et al. 2006), require at least one backward and one forward citation pass that records: (a) major critiques or null-finding replications; (b) domain-adjacent studies that report weaker or contrary effects (e.g. tenure-performance meta-analyses with small CEO effects, studies finding no structural-inertia penalties).
2. **Non-English search round.** Add a mandatory German- and French-language search step for institutional-longevity and parliamentary-tenure literatures (continental studies of parliamentary mandates, term limits, bureaucratic tenure), anchored on EU and national-parliament research services.
3. **"Hostile-to-thesis" band.** For FH-005 in particular, require at least one band of sources that explicitly argue **against** growing executive-tenure volatility or against AI-induced coordination brittleness — e.g. work finding stable or increasing CEO tenure in some markets, or governance reports arguing AI strengthens institutional resilience when properly regulated. These should be summarised in a short falsification-tracker entry rather than left implicit.

With these tightenings, the TC-11 search strategy meets a reasonable standard for selection-bias control in a scoping-document context.

### 5.2 Spencer Stuart [12] — methodological verdict

**Verdict:** Spencer Stuart [12] **does not, on its own, qualify as robust evidence for H₁ second-order signatures once industry-mix, M&A, and pandemic effects are considered.** It remains highly valuable as a current descriptive practitioner source.

Public documentation establishes that the figures (168 new CEOs; 84% first-time enterprise; average incoming age 54.4; average tenure 8.5 years, down from 9.1 in 2021) are based on a comprehensive scan of S&P 1500 companies, with clearly stated counts by index segment and broad industry group. The numbers are consistently reproduced in Spencer Stuart's own communications and in independent commentary. However:

- **Industry-mix controls.** Public report segments transitions by broad industry but does not present variance-decomposition or regression analyses showing whether observed shifts persist after controlling for changing index composition, sector-specific shocks, or differing baseline turnover rates across industries.
- **M&A and restructuring controls.** The report notes elevated transitions amid market volatility, inflation, activism, and technological change but does not clearly separate ordinary board-driven transitions from those triggered by mergers, spinoffs, or bankruptcies. Tail behaviour of tenure distributions may partly reflect episodic corporate-finance events rather than underlying changes in tenure regimes.
- **Pandemic-era extraordinary turnover.** Spencer Stuart situates 2025 as the highest turnover year since 2010 but does not fully decompose whether the 8.5-year average reflects reversion from pandemic-related anomalies (e.g. delayed retirements 2020–2021) or a new equilibrium.

**Required v0.2 adjustments:**

- Use [12] to document **current levels and patterns** (e.g. "in 2025, 168 new S&P 1500 CEOs were appointed, with an average tenure of about 8.5 years and a high share of first-time CEOs"), explicitly labelling it as practitioner data.
- Refrain from using [12] as primary or sole basis for claims about **variance growth, kurtosis, or reversal frequency** in CEO tenure. Such claims should instead be anchored in academic series (Kaplan & Minton; CEO-effect variance studies; Russell Reynolds index) with [12] as triangulating point.
- Where the memo currently treats [12] as evidence **for** H₁ second-order signatures, v0.2 should either (a) soften the language to "prima facie consistent with" and note the lack of controls, or (b) move the point into a clearly demarcated practitioner-insight box.
